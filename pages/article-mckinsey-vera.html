How the McKinsey Manufacturing Workforce Article Supports VERA

The achievement gap is the workforce gap — identified twelve years too late

Source: McKinsey & Company · February 27, 2026 | Analysis

← Back to Articles

The oral-written deficit is the manufacturing workforce crisis — one level upstream

The McKinsey article's central finding is that manufacturers cannot find workers with the skills they need, and that the problem must be addressed before middle school.

What it does not say — but VERA's data makes explicit — is that the English Learner population disproportionately feeds the manufacturing workforce in California, Texas, and the deep south.

A student with strong oral skills but weak written and analytical skills can operate a forklift. They cannot manage a fleet of autonomous guided vehicles, program a digital twin, or read a maintenance manual for military aircraft. The oral-written delta is precisely the gap between the worker that manufacturing already has and the worker that manufacturing now needs.

The $2/$3/$5 ratio is a VERA argument

The article states that for every $2 invested in digital, manufacturers must invest $3 in process optimization and $5 in talent and change management.

K-12 is the $5.

California is spending $142 billion a year on K-12, with $8–10 billion specifically targeted at high-need students. VERA's LCAP match rate question — is the money reaching the students who generated it? — is exactly the ROI question that manufacturers are now demanding from their own talent investments.

The language of "talent ROI" that McKinsey is applying to shop floors applies directly to the LCAP accountability framework.

"If I don't get somebody interested in manufacturing by middle school, we've missed them." — Major US Manufacturer, Georgia Workforce Summit (quoted in McKinsey article)

"If I don't get somebody by middle school, we've missed them"

This quote from the Georgia manufacturer is the single most important line in the article for VERA's purposes.

It precisely validates VERA's intervention logic. Type 4 students — high oral, low written — are identifiable in 3rd and 4th grade from CAASPP and ELPAC data.

That is when the intervention window is open. By high school, the sorting has already happened. The manufacturer is describing, from the demand side, exactly the problem VERA is designed to surface from the supply side.

Automation is changing the skill floor, not eliminating the need for workers

The article is emphatic: jobs will change, workers will remain.

The new manufacturing job requires reading, writing, analysis, and digital literacy — not just manual dexterity. A student who exits K-12 with strong oral English but weak written English is not prepared for the technician role; they are prepared for the role that the technician's automation is replacing.

VERA's Type 4 identification is, in this frame, an economic sorting mechanism. The students VERA flags today are the ones who will be sorted into the half of the economy AI hollows out — unless someone intervenes.

The regional talent pool argument maps to VERA's district-level instrument

The article's prescription for manufacturing — stop competing for the same small talent pool, build regional workforce pipelines together — is structurally identical to VERA's LCAP accountability argument.

A COE that can see the oral-written delta across all districts in its county can coordinate intervention the same way FAME coordinates apprenticeships across 500 companies. VERA is the instrument that makes the regional talent pipeline visible before workers reach the shop floor.

The synthesis

Manufacturing has named the problem from the demand side. VERA names it from the supply side. The gap is the same gap.

The student California is failing to bring to written proficiency in 4th grade is the worker that Georgia's manufacturers cannot find at 24.

The distance between those two facts is twelve years and a $142 billion annual budget that is not being held accountable for closing it.

The McKinsey article provides third-party validation from the most credible management consultancy in the world, speaking directly to the economic consequences of the achievement gap.

CSBA named the problem. VERA built the instrument to measure it.
