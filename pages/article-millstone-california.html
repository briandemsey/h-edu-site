The Millstone We Built

California's Achievement Gap, Fifty Years of Failure, and the AI Reckoning We Can No Longer Defer

By Brian Demsey | March 2026

← Back to Articles

Fifty Years. One Direction. Down.

We have been trying to close the achievement gap since the early 1970s — and failing. Not quietly, not narrowly, but comprehensively and at scale. Half a century of blue-ribbon commissions, billion-dollar initiatives, and well-meaning reforms, and we are still standing in essentially the same place. Worse, the world has fundamentally changed around us while the gap has not.

The numbers are not in dispute. In 1978, California's per-pupil spending on public education exceeded the national average. Our schools were, by most measures, among the strongest in the country. Today, California ranks 38th among states in math and 33rd in reading on the National Assessment of Educational Progress. We fell from near the top to the bottom third. It happened slowly, then all at once, and we kept funding the same failing approaches as it happened.

$23 Billion Federal pandemic relief spent on K-12 with no reliable mechanism to measure what it bought

The pandemic made things worse, but the pandemic is not the story. NAEP and SBAC data both show substantial progress for high-performing students — while those at the bottom have seen continued, and even accelerating, decline. The gap between California's highest- and lowest-performing students has been widening for a decade, pandemic or not.

The specific numbers for our most vulnerable students should stop every policymaker cold. Black students in California achieve English proficiency at 24%, Latino students at 37%. In math, Black students reach proficiency at 18% and Latino students at 30%. Low-income students achieve English proficiency at 37% — roughly half the rate of moderate- and high-income households.

Those are not statistics. Those are children.

The Gap Was Never Two-Dimensional

The achievement gap has always been measured on two axes — language and mathematics. That framing served the industrial economy, where those two variables largely determined whether a child would participate in economic life or be excluded from it. The framing is now obsolete.

There is a third variable, and its absence from the gap definition is the most expensive oversight in American education policy. AI literacy — the ability to direct, interrogate, verify, and critically evaluate AI systems — is now as foundational to economic participation as reading was in 1970. A child who graduates unable to read is disqualified from the knowledge economy. A child who graduates unable to work with AI tools is disqualified from the economy that is replacing it.

The cruelty of this moment is that the third variable is, in principle, the most democratizing of the three. A child with fragile literacy can still learn to prompt. A child with shaky arithmetic can still learn to evaluate AI output critically. The same tools that are eliminating the entry-level jobs that historically absorbed underprepared workers are also — if deployed correctly — the most powerful tutoring and translation infrastructure ever built.

The gap contains its own instrument of repair. We are simply not using it.

And Now Comes AI

For generations, the implicit social contract was this: if a child struggled academically, there were still rungs on the ladder. Manufacturing, service work, entry-level office jobs — a path forward that didn't require mastery of advanced literacy or mathematics. That contract is being voided in real time.

Jobs requiring lower levels of skill or training are precisely where AI is producing the sharpest reductions in hiring. Postings for entry-level jobs in the U.S. plunged 35% from January 2023 to June 2025. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has forecast that 50% of entry-level jobs may be eliminated as the technology improves. What we once called a safety net of low-barrier employment is becoming a trap door.

The students we are failing today — the 63% of California's low-income students who cannot meet grade-level standards in English, the 75% who cannot meet them in math — will arrive at adulthood in a labor market that has no structural tolerance for low literacy or low numeracy. The rungs are gone. The ladder has no bottom.

"If I don't get somebody interested in manufacturing by middle school, we've missed them." — Major US Manufacturer, Georgia Workforce Summit

The Graveyard Silicon Valley Built

Silicon Valley has tried to solve this before. The record is not encouraging. Mark Zuckerberg's $100 million gift to Newark's schools produced, by most accounts, nothing measurable. AltSchool burned through venture capital engineering a platform for the already-advantaged. Summit Schools scaled a model that worked beautifully for motivated students and had limited purchase on the population that actually needs intervention.

The pattern is consistent: Silicon Valley arrives as a savior, optimizes for the metrics it knows how to measure — engagement, retention, platform growth — and exits when the unit economics don't pencil or the attention moves on. The district is left with a three-year contract, declining scores, and a lesson about the difference between disruption and improvement.

A Fortune investigation published this month found something that should close the argument about technology-as-pedagogy: the generation educated most heavily with classroom technology is the first in modern history to test below its parents. Screen time in classrooms correlates negatively with learning outcomes across the PISA dataset. The valley spent 25 years telling schools they were broken and that computers could fix them. The diagnosis was manufactured. The cure made the patient sicker.

The Ask That Has Never Been Made

And yet Silicon Valley must be part of the answer. The question is which part.

Silicon Valley is the best infrastructure builder on earth. What it cannot do — and what decades of evidence confirms it should not try to do — is replace the relationship between a child and a teacher who knows their name. The ask that has always failed is "fix education." The ask that has never been made is: build the instrument panel for the people who do.

What does that instrument panel look like?

  • Real-time language translation that removes the barrier facing California's 1.1 million English learners without requiring a single curriculum change
  • A closed feedback loop that connects assessment results to intervention records to observable outcomes — so that when a district spends $4 million on a literacy program, someone can tell you in 90 days whether it is working
  • A parent-observer interface, available in every home language, that captures the behavioral signals no teacher ever sees: the child who stopped talking about school, who is sleeping too much, who has gone quiet
  • AI tutoring deployed as supplemental acceleration, not as a replacement for instruction — precisely because AI is most effective when the learner brings foundational knowledge to the interaction

None of these are pedagogy. All of them are engineering. That is the lane.

The Supply Chain Argument

The business case is not philanthropic. It is supply chain. Google, Apple, Salesforce, and every major employer headquartered between San Jose and San Francisco will face a structural talent shortage within a decade if the bottom 60 percent of California's students remain functionally underprepared for an AI-driven labor market.

The pipeline runs through Compton and Fresno and East Salinas. The engineers who will build the next generation of AI systems are sitting in classrooms right now where 75 percent of their peers cannot meet grade-level math standards.

This is not a social justice argument, though it is that too. It is a supply chain argument. The workforce that Silicon Valley needs to remain competitive is being lost in a system that the valley helped break and has not yet helped fix.

Build Them the Cockpit

This is not a failure of resources. It is not a failure of intention. It is a failure of instrumentation. We have been flying a $100 billion system without a cockpit.

The instrument panel does not fly the rocket. It gives the people who do the data they need to fly it well. Apollo Mission Control did not go to the moon. Twelve men did — because 400,000 engineers built the system that told them exactly where they were.

California's teachers are not the problem. They are the astronauts. They are doing the most demanding work in the state with the least reliable instrumentation of any high-stakes profession in existence. A surgeon has real-time vital signs. A pilot has a cockpit. A portfolio manager has a Bloomberg terminal. A third-grade teacher in Watts has last year's standardized test scores and her own judgment.

Build them the cockpit. That is the ask. It is specific. It is bounded. It is measurable.

The window is not theoretical. California's AI in Education Workgroup will issue a model policy in July. The vocabulary established in that document — what "verification" means, what "closed-loop accountability" means, what "parent engagement" means in a system with AI instrumentation — will shape procurement decisions across 1,000 school districts for the next decade.

Whoever shapes that vocabulary is at the table before the concrete sets. The table is open right now.

Fifty years was the warning. AI is the deadline.
